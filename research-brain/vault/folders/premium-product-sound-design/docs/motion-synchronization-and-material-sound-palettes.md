# Motion Synchronization and Material Sound Palettes

Sound should follow motion, but not every movement deserves its own effect. Premium work supports the shape of motion rather than tracing every frame.

The useful timing model is:

1. **Anticipation:** a tiny pre-motion air cue or tonal lift.
2. **Contact:** the actual touch, click, open, stop, or physical event.
3. **Settle:** a small post-hit weight cue that makes the product feel grounded.
4. **Decay:** the tail that places the event inside a believable space.

If a movement has no physical consequence on screen, the sound should usually be reduced to ambience or omitted.

## Camera-Movement Standards

| Camera movement | Best sound approach | Intensity | Common mistake | Prompt wording |
|---|---|---:|---|---|
| Slow dolly-in | Low, soft air movement or tonal swell; no obvious whoosh unless speed is visible | Low | Trailer-style rise for a tiny move | "subtle low whoosh matching the slow camera push-in, restrained and elegant" |
| Lateral tracking | Light stereo pass-by, soft surface presence, ambient continuity | Low-medium | Hard pan that feels gimmicky on headphones or phones | "gentle stereo motion that follows the lateral camera track without exaggeration" |
| Camera orbit | Shifting airy texture and changing material highlights | Low | Repeating identical whoosh loop each quarter turn | "subtle rotating air movement and changing surface detail as the camera orbits" |
| Macro close-up | Intimate, close tactile micro-foley, reduced room | Low | Huge cinematic boom on tiny object movement | "close-miked tactile detail, very subtle room, precise micro-foley" |
| Rack focus | Usually no discrete SFX, or only a faint tonal shimmer if brand-appropriate | Very low | Sci-fi focus noise on every focus pull | "very subtle tonal shift as focus changes, no artificial sci-fi effect" |
| Fast transition | Short, clean transition with a matched tail into next ambience | Medium | Overlong whoosh that masks the cut point | "clean transition accent with a short tail, aligned to the cut, not oversized" |
| Static hero shot | Ambience plus tiny material presence; let silence carry authority | Very low | Filling stillness because silence feels empty | "quiet premium studio ambience, almost still, with subtle product presence" |
| Top-down reveal | Soft lift, fabric friction, or cardboard friction depending on material | Low-medium | Generic swoosh unrelated to material | "soft reveal accent driven by the product material, not a generic whoosh" |
| Vertical reel montage | Faster attack, shorter tails, cleaner transient structure for mobile | Medium | Dense low-end and long tails that blur on phones | "clean, short commercial accents designed for mobile clarity" |

## Shot Scale

Sound perspective should match the image:

- **Macro shots:** close-miked detail, less room, more tactile surface information.
- **Wide shots:** more air, less tactile detail, less intimate foley.
- **Static hero shots:** ambience and silence can create authority.
- **Fast social edits:** shorter tails and clean transients are safer on mobile speakers.

## Paper, Books, Flyers, and Booklets

Prioritize:

- soft page turns
- smooth paper slides
- gentle taps
- fold and unfold textures
- subtle print-room tonality when the brand context supports it

The sound should suggest fiber and surface resistance, not sharp plastic crackle. White-paper or premium print products usually need softer transients and less low-end than cardboard.

Useful prompt fragments:

- "soft tactile paper slide"
- "gentle premium page turn"
- "subtle print-shop texture in the background"
- "quiet paper fold with natural decay"

## Kraft Packaging, Boxes, and Bags

Build around:

- cardboard friction
- lid release
- soft fold movement
- fiber texture
- warm low-mid presence

Kraft should feel tactile and human. Transient edges should be softened slightly and supported by warm ambience rather than sharp tech-style clicks.

Avoid making a small kraft bag sound like a shipping crate.

Useful prompt fragments:

- "warm tactile cardboard friction"
- "soft kraft bag movement"
- "subtle box lid release"
- "natural paper fibre texture"

## Glass, Perfume, and Cosmetics

Use sparse, precise cues:

- light glass resonance
- refined cap click
- delicate liquid movement
- tiny droplet behavior when visible
- elegant upper harmonic detail only when physically believable

Do not use fantasy sparkles by default. Luxury glass usually sounds quiet, tight, and controlled. Magical shimmer unrelated to contact, refraction, or physical movement makes it feel fake.

Useful prompt fragments:

- "quiet glass resonance as the bottle turns"
- "precise magnetic cap click"
- "subtle liquid shift inside the bottle"
- "clean delicate glass contact, no magical sparkle"

## Metal, Watches, and Tech Products

Prioritize precision over loudness.

Watch audio should suggest engineered tolerances:

- tiny ticks
- clasp clicks
- brushed-metal friction
- crown movement
- magnetic closure

Tech products can use restrained UI ticks, servo-like slides, and polished electro-mechanical cues only when physically plausible.

Avoid giant sub booms for small devices and bright sci-fi chirps unless the brand explicitly wants that tone.

Useful prompt fragments:

- "precise metallic click"
- "tight brushed metal movement"
- "premium micro-mechanical ticks"
- "controlled electromechanical motion, no oversized boom"

## Plastic and Matte Products

Show shape and contact through:

- soft taps
- restrained clicks
- low-noise movement

Avoid the cheap-toy signature: thin, bright, hollow, and too loud. Matte plastic usually wants controlled high frequencies and compact decay.

Useful prompt fragments:

- "soft controlled matte plastic tap"
- "quiet tactile click"
- "restrained low-noise movement"

## Fabric, Leather, and Soft Materials

Use:

- friction
- weave movement
- leather creak
- subtle texture

These materials feel premium when the mix leaves headroom and lets micro-detail carry the realism. If they are too bright or too forward, they start to sound synthetic.

Useful prompt fragments:

- "subtle cloth friction"
- "soft leather creak"
- "fine textile texture, close and restrained"

## Coffee and Cafe Products

Combine:

- beans
- grind texture
- steam
- cup placement
- sleeve friction
- tear-open packaging
- warm cafe air bed

Coffee reads through context as much as object sound. The goal is comfortable warmth, not cafe chaos.

Useful prompt fragments:

- "warm cafe ambience"
- "soft ceramic cup placement"
- "subtle steam and coffee bean texture"
- "paper cup sleeve friction with natural warmth"
