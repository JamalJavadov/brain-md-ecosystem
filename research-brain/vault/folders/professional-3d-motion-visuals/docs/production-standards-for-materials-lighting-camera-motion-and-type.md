# Production Standards for Materials, Lighting, Camera, Motion, and Type

The useful shift is to convert abstract taste into production standards. Premium 3D and AI-assisted visuals should be built from measurable decisions: material properties, light logic, camera intent, motion timing, and typography hierarchy.

## Studio-Level Standards

| Discipline | Studio-level standard | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Materials** | Use PBR-first material stacks: base colour, metallic or specular logic, roughness, normal, and height or displacement where needed. Add micro-bevels to every hero hard-surface asset. Avoid full-scene perfect gloss; introduce roughness variation and edge history. | Realism comes from how materials respond to light. Viewers may forgive stylisation, but they rarely forgive materials that behave impossibly. |
| **Lighting** | Build motivated key, fill, rim, or product-studio setups. Use area lights where soft borders are desired, and adjust light size intentionally. Keep glow and volumetrics subordinate to form. | Premium lighting feels directional and authored. Cheap lighting feels like a filter. |
| **Camera** | Specify shot size, focal intent, movement path, and lens behaviour. Use depth of field as an optical choice, not as a universal premium effect. Lock hero angles for products, then cut to purposeful detail views. | The camera should reveal hierarchy: first read, second read, proof detail, brand sign-off. Without hierarchy, movement feels random. |
| **Motion** | Design motion beats in an animatic before generation. Use anticipation, slow in and slow out, arcs, follow-through, and controlled cadence. Refine timing with curve tools rather than linear keyframes. | Random drift is one of the fastest ways to make a shot feel AI-made. Purposeful timing makes even simple movement feel expensive. |
| **Typography** | Build a type system before the visuals: font pairing, scale rules, tracking behaviour, alignment logic, and grid. Check contrast even in muted premium palettes. | Text is usually where cheapness appears first. Premium visuals tolerate bold style only when the message stays crisp and legible. |
| **Text in 3D scenes** | Treat text either as a real 3D object inside the world or as a separate 2D overlay in post. Do not ask the model to invent the scene and finalise critical copy at the same time. | Brand names, product names, and service labels are too important to leave to systems with known text-rendering weaknesses. |

## Material Rules

For every hero material, define:

- physical name, not mood word;
- base colour;
- roughness behaviour;
- metallic or specular logic;
- normal or micro-surface detail;
- height, displacement, emboss, grain, or machining detail where relevant;
- edge treatment;
- thickness;
- contact behaviour with nearby surfaces.

Examples of physical material names:

- brushed champagne aluminium;
- anodised copper;
- frosted glass;
- smoked acrylic;
- matte ceramic;
- uncoated paperboard;
- soft-touch laminate;
- spot UV label;
- foil accent;
- coated and uncoated paper stock with visible grain.

Avoid defining a material only as "luxury", "premium", "futuristic", or "cinematic."

## Lighting Rules

Start by deciding what the light must prove.

- Texture-led lighting should reveal fibre, grain, pores, scratches, emboss, machining lines, or paper edge quality.
- Reflection-led lighting should shape glass, metal, acrylic, gloss coating, and curved edges.
- Silhouette-led lighting should separate dark products, black-on-black scenes, or premium low-key compositions.
- Brand-led lighting should support the brand palette and not fight the typography.

Use glow, bloom, haze, and volumetrics only when they support form or depth. They should not be the source of premium perception.

## Camera Rules

Every camera move needs a job.

Good reasons for camera movement:

- reveal the logo;
- prove material finish;
- show product thickness;
- demonstrate assembly;
- transition from hook to proof;
- create a brand-owned final composition.

Weak reasons:

- "make it cinematic";
- constant orbiting;
- random push-ins;
- motion that hides unreadable details;
- shallow depth of field used to disguise weak composition.

## Motion Rules

Motion should feel patient, tactile, and precise.

Useful motion vocabulary:

- glides;
- reveals;
- weighted rotations;
- assembly motions;
- sliding mechanisms;
- controlled camera moves;
- light sweeps across bevels;
- material transitions tied to product function.

Avoid:

- perpetual drift;
- random orbiting;
- shapeless morphs;
- chaotic pacing;
- floating elements with no reason;
- logo melting or deformation;
- motion that competes with text readability.

## Typography Rules

Typography must be planned before final visuals.

Hard rules:

- Do not let generative models own final brand text.
- Composite final copy manually whenever possible.
- Use a grid for alignment.
- Define hierarchy before animation.
- Keep service labels short.
- Check contrast deliberately.
- Do not mix languages carelessly; language switching should signal strategy, not indecision.
- If text lives on a panel, card, or surface, that surface needs perspective, spacing, and interaction cues that prove it belongs to the scene.

## Product Ad Structure

A practical premium product-ad pattern:

1. **Concept-defining hero frame:** establishes the campaign idea and visual language.
2. **Proof shots:** reveal product design, material, mechanism, service value, or craftsmanship.
3. **Brand-owned closing image:** resolves to a controlled logo, product, or call-to-action frame.

The best product visuals are not just about beautiful objects. They are about a specific reason the object, service, or brand matters.
