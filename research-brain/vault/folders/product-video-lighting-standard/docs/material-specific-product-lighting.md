# Material-Specific Product Lighting

Material realism depends on lighting behavior. The goal is not to make every product brighter; it is to reveal the right surface cues for that product.

## Paper, Books, Flyers, and Booklets

Paper looks premium when it has both clarity and topography.

Use:

- A large soft top or top-front source for legibility.
- A restrained low-angle side or grazing accent to reveal grain, embossing, page edges, folds, print relief, and paper thickness.
- Diffuse even light when the main priority is accurate print viewing.
- Glare control, especially on white paper and coated stock.

Source detail to preserve:

- Raking light is illumination at an oblique or nearly parallel angle that reveals surface topography.
- Glare on prints is reduced when light strikes at roughly 30 degrees from vertical.

White paper should stay bright but below clipping so texture and ink coverage remain visible.

Avoid:

- Blown white paper.
- Hard glare crossing typography.
- Shadows that obscure text blocks.
- Overly sterile paper with no page depth.

## Kraft Packaging, Paper Bags, Boxes, and Brown Stock

Kraft stock benefits from warm-neutral side light and soft top fill because its useful detail lives in texture, edge wear, folds, and fibers.

Use:

- Broad soft source from above-front.
- Warmer directional key from one side.
- Very restrained fill so brown material does not become flat or muddy.
- Negative fill when extra contrast is needed.

Avoid:

- Overly cool white balance, which can make beige stock look dirty.
- Flat frontal light.
- Overexposed paper fibers.
- Random color shifts between shots.

Prompt cue:

> photoreal kraft paper bag, warm side light revealing paper texture, soft top fill, subtle natural falloff, tactile folds, grounded shadow

## Glass, Perfume, and Cosmetics

Reflective transparent objects are usually lit by shaping what they reflect, not by pointing uncontrolled light at them.

Use:

- Clean rim highlights first.
- Vertical strip reflections for glass edges and contour lines.
- Small white cards or weak bounce for controlled fill.
- Black cards or strip masks to prevent milky glare.
- Subtle internal glow or label texture only after the edge structure works.
- Cross-polarisation logic when glare must be reduced and liquid revealed.

Practical rule:

Design clean rim highlights first, then add subtle internal glow or label texture, then open shadows only if needed.

Avoid:

- Milky glare.
- Over-bloom.
- Neon glow.
- Random duplicated reflections.
- Broad frontal wash.
- Floating bottles without grounding.

Prompt cue:

> photoreal glass perfume bottle, clean vertical strip reflections, narrow rim highlights on the glass edges, controlled luxury studio lighting, visible liquid and refraction, stable reflections

## Metal, Watches, and Tech Products

Polished metal needs broad diffused reflections for realism and sharply targeted accents for micro-detail.

Use:

- Broad diffused reflections to make metal read as shaped light on metal, not flat gray.
- White boards to recycle top light into the case or bevels.
- Rim light for clean contour separation.
- Black papers, flags, or cards for extra crispness.
- Precise hard accents for dial features, indices, chamfers, buttons, and machining details.
- Large soft sources for clean technology-product contours, controlled with black flags to prevent flare.

Silver should look like shaped light on gray metal, not a flat gray material with random white streaks. Gold should show warm controlled reflections rather than saturated yellow mud.

Avoid:

- Noisy reflections.
- Flat gray metal.
- Soft unreadable watch dials.
- Unstable highlight crawl.
- Logo flare.
- Rainbow reflections unless the brand or material specifically calls for them.

Prompt cue:

> photoreal luxury watch macro, diffused top light, crisp contour edge light, controlled micro-reflections on polished metal, readable dial detail, precision studio setup

## Plastic and Matte Products

Matte materials often fail because they are front-lit too evenly. They need soft gradients and selective specular control, not full gloss or total flatness.

Use:

- One broad key from above-front or around 45 degrees.
- Lower-powered bounce or fill to keep label readability.
- Restrained rim to separate edges.
- Smooth gradients that reveal form.

Avoid:

- Dead frontal wash.
- Cheap glossy hotspots.
- Over-bloom.
- Neon-colored edge light that makes plastic feel synthetically wrong.

Prompt cue:

> photoreal matte product, broad soft key from above-front, restrained edge separation, smooth material gradients, readable label, controlled non-glossy highlights

## Fabric, Leather, and Soft Materials

Texture lives in directional relief.

Use:

- Directional side or back-side key to reveal weave, pores, grain, stitching, textile straps, or soft-pouch folds.
- Enough fill to keep shadows from collapsing into featureless black.
- Grazing light when surface topography is the hero detail.

Avoid:

- Straight-on lighting that makes texture flat.
- Crushed blacks that hide grain.
- Overly broad fill that erases weave or leather relief.

Prompt cue:

> directional side light revealing leather grain and stitching, soft restrained fill, grounded shadow, tactile premium material response

## White-on-White and Black-on-Black Products

Products placed against similar-value backgrounds require explicit separation strategy.

For white-on-white:

- Preserve product edges.
- Use separate object light and background light.
- Keep subtle grounding shadow.
- Avoid pushing both background and product to pure white.

For black-on-black:

- Preserve contour separation.
- Use narrow rim or controlled edge highlights.
- Keep hero surfaces readable.
- Avoid broad muddy fill and unreadable silhouette blobs.

## Material Lighting Review

Before approving a material shot, check:

- Paper and print: texture visible, whites not clipped, text readable, glare controlled.
- Kraft stock: warm-neutral rendering, fold texture visible, brown looks natural rather than dirty.
- Glass: edge highlights clean, label readable, liquid visible, glare not milky.
- Metal: reflections shaped, not random; detail accents controlled; color neutral and credible.
- Matte plastic: gradients visible, no dead front-flat wash, no cheap glossy hotspots.
- Fabric and leather: directional relief visible, texture not crushed in black.
- White-on-white products: edge separation preserved.
- Black-on-black products: contour separation preserved.
