# Product Lighting Checklists and Quality Control

Use these checklists as production gates before prompting, look development, rendering, editing, and final approval.

## Pre-Production Lighting Checklist

- Define the hero surface first: paper, glass, metal, matte plastic, fabric, kraft, or mixed material.
- Decide the lighting family before anything else: high-key clean, low-key luxury, warm lifestyle, cool technical, or editorial neutral.
- Lock the dominant color temperature for the scene.
- Choose the main product-reading angle before refining reflections.
- Decide whether the shot is reflection-led, silhouette-led, or texture-led.
- If using AI generation, gather clean reference images on plain backgrounds.
- Decide what must remain readable during movement: logo, label, emboss, crown, cap, stitching, dial, or page edge.

## 3D Scene Lighting Checklist

- Start with one key and one support element before adding accents.
- Use area lights or softbox equivalents as the default for product keys.
- Add strip lights only when a linear reflection or contour is needed.
- Keep HDRI or environment light subordinate to the hero light unless the shot is explicitly environment-driven.
- Add black cards or flags before increasing contrast with more raw intensity.
- Enable contact shadows or equivalent grounding where the product meets a surface.
- Check glossy and metallic objects from the actual camera view, not only from viewport orbit.
- Test the lighting in clay first if materials are still in flux.
- Keep bloom restrained and purposeful.
- Verify that the brightest point in frame is the correct point in frame.

## AI Prompt Lighting Checklist

- State the product clearly.
- State the environment clearly.
- State the key-light direction clearly.
- State light softness explicitly.
- State color temperature or warm/cool intent explicitly.
- Describe reflections in material-specific language.
- Add a continuity instruction such as stable studio lighting.
- Use ingredients or saved frames for repeatability.
- Avoid contradictory instructions between text and references.
- Prefer descriptive negative terms over "no/don't" phrasing for Veo-style negatives.
- If iterating in Flow, use follow-up prompt turns to refine only one lighting variable at a time.

## Material Lighting Checklist

- **Paper and print:** texture visible, whites not clipped, text readable, glare controlled.
- **Kraft stock:** warm-neutral rendering, fold texture visible, brown looks natural rather than dirty.
- **Glass:** edge highlights clean, label readable, liquid visible, glare not milky.
- **Metal:** reflections shaped, not random; detail accents controlled; color neutral credible.
- **Matte plastic:** gradients visible, no dead front-flat wash, no cheap glossy hotspots.
- **Fabric and leather:** directional relief visible, texture not crushed in black.
- **White-on-white products:** edge separation preserved.
- **Black-on-black products:** contour separation preserved.

## Shot-to-Shot Continuity Checklist

- Is the key still coming from the same side?
- Has highlight shape changed without a story reason?
- Has the product white balance shifted shot to shot?
- Are black levels and shadow density consistent?
- Are reflections stable across cuts?
- Does the product keep the same apparent material identity in every shot?
- On waveform, are hero whites and blacks staying in the same family?
- On vectorscope, are neutrals drifting?

## Vertical Delivery Checklist

- Is the product composed for 9:16 first, rather than cropped from a wider frame?
- Are the hero product, brightest highlight path, and most important branding inside the central safe region?
- Are text overlays and calls to action protected from Reels, Stories, TikTok, or other UI overlays?
- Are gradients subtle enough to survive platform compression?
- Are blacks lifted enough for mobile viewing without destroying the low-key mood?
- Are specular highlights controlled enough to avoid compression breakup?

## Final Quality-Control Checklist

The final question is not whether the shot is flashy. It is whether it reads like a real studio commercial.

- Is the product clearly visible within the first second?
- Is the product color accurate to brand references?
- Are shadows physically believable rather than generic ambient grey?
- Are reflections shaped and controlled?
- Is lighting direction consistent from frame to frame and from shot to shot?
- Does the product feel grounded by contact shadow, reflection, or both?
- Is the scene bright enough for mobile viewing without looking overlit?
- Is there excessive glow, bloom, or flare?
- Do gradients survive compression cleanly?
- Are text overlays and CTAs safe from UI overlap in 9:16 delivery?
- Does the video look like a real studio commercial rather than an effect demo?
- Do any highlights, shadows, colors, or reflections reveal an AI-generated look?

If the workflow uses Google Flow, remember that invisible SynthID watermarking still applies even when the image itself is visually convincing.

## Final Rule

Use one dominant light story, one material strategy, and one continuity logic.

For premium 3D and AI-assisted product work, the target look is not "more cinematic"; it is more coherent. Coherent light direction, material response, color temperature, reflections, and continuity are what make a shot feel expensive and believable.
