# Commercial Video Palette Library and Review Workflow

A reusable color system lets designers, 3D artists, AI prompt writers, and editors work from the same vocabulary. The most efficient workflow is to create a color brief before storyboarding.

That brief should include business category, emotional goal, dominant/support/accent palette, forbidden colors, material notes, lighting temperature, text contrast choices, and platform target.

## 20-Second Vertical Video Color Plan

A five-scene structure of roughly four seconds each is workable for both AI and motion workflows because each scene has concise visual logic.

| Scene | Purpose | Color strategy | Lighting strategy | Text strategy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Product reveal | Establish hero and mood | Use dominant neutral base; reveal accent only after silhouette or first highlight | Clean key, strong edge separation, minimal color contamination | Very little or no text |
| Feature highlight | Show benefit or mechanism | Introduce support color in controlled graphic elements | Keep same white balance; add one focused highlight | One short benefit line with clear contrast |
| Material or detail close-up | Sell surface quality | Reduce palette complexity further; let material color dominate | Macro-friendly specular control, soft shadows | Small labels only if necessary |
| Brand or service message | Broaden from product to offer | Return to full palette family but keep background quiet | Slightly flatter lighting if logos or packaging must read perfectly | Main service statement; one accent word maximum |
| Final CTA | Convert attention into action | Highest contrast moment in the video | Stable lighting, clean background, no competing props | CTA in strongest text/background pairing, kept clear of UI-safe areas |

## Team Workflow Checklist

| Stage | Questions the team should answer |
|---|---|
| Before designing | What must the viewer feel in the first two seconds? What is the non-negotiable product color? Which category cues are expected? Which colors are forbidden because they cheapen, confuse, or reduce trust? |
| During 3D scene creation | Does the background separate the product by value? Are reflections clean? Are metal, glass, paper, plastic, and fabric reading correctly? Is the accent color too large? |
| During AI prompting | Is the palette limited? Are product color and material explicitly locked? Is the background defined? Is lighting temperature specified? Have random color shifts and excessive glow been forbidden? |
| During editing and grading | Do scopes confirm stable whites and blacks? Is saturation controlled after transitions? Does every text frame meet readable contrast? Are shot-to-shot hues drifting? |
| Before sending to client | Does the palette still feel like one brand? Is the CTA readable on a phone? Does the video still feel premium when compressed? Could the client reuse this palette across future videos? |

Review standard: reject any shot that wins style but loses product truth. In commercial work, accurate product color, clear hierarchy, and repeatable brand tone matter more than a single impressive frame.

## Final Rules for Premium Color Use

- Choose color by business meaning first, taste second. Congruity beats randomness.
- Limit the palette to one dominant color, one support color, one accent, and disciplined neutrals.
- Protect product truth. If product color drifts, the video fails commercially even if it looks artistic.
- Use value contrast before adding more hues.
- Let materials influence color decisions. Metal, glass, paper, plastic, and fabric do not carry color the same way.
- Treat lighting as part of color design. White balance, color temperature, and reflections can make the same swatch feel luxurious or wrong.
- Use neon as an accent, not a full visual lifestyle.
- For mobile video, readability is a design requirement. Check contrast, safe zones, and compression behavior.
- Do not rely on color alone to communicate. Pair color cues with placement, type, icons, or wording.
- In AI workflows, write the palette into the prompt.
- Short controlled shots beat long chaotic shots.
- A premium palette stays stable across scenes. Change framing, motion, and detail; do not reinvent the brand every four seconds.

Operating principle: every commercial video needs a written color brief before generation or rendering starts. Once that exists, color harmony, psychology, product clarity, text readability, lighting, and grading become easier to judge, document, and repeat.
